The Case

Why Europe Must
Become a Federation

The case is not sentimental. It follows from a clear-eyed reading of what coordination without authority has cost, and continues to cost, the people of Europe.

Europe has spent eighty years building institutions that are strong enough to bind their members to a common market, a common currency for nineteen of them, and a growing body of common law — and yet remain institutionally incapable of making a binding foreign-policy decision, raising a common defence budget, or setting a fiscal policy commensurate with a currency area of its size. The argument that follows is that this is not a design flaw to be patched; it is the predictable consequence of a structure that was never completed.

I. The Historical Argument

The idea that a united Europe would permanently foreclose the possibility of major European war is as old as the post-war settlement itself. Altiero Spinelli and Ernesto Rossi, interned by Mussolini on the island of Ventotene, wrote as early as 1941 that the nation-state system was the "main cause of the ills from which our society suffers."

Their solution was not better-managed sovereignty but its pooling: a federal authority with real powers, real revenue, and real democratic accountability.

Robert Schuman's declaration of 9 May 1950 — drafted in large part by Jean Monnet — translated this impulse into practical statecraft. The proposal to pool coal and steel production under a common High Authority was explicitly designed to make war between France and Germany "not merely unthinkable, but materially impossible."

This was not an economic measure dressed up as politics — it was a security architecture expressed through economic means.

II. The Economic Argument

The eurozone crisis of 2010–2015 demonstrated with painful clarity what happens when a monetary union operates without a fiscal union. Seventeen countries sharing a currency had no common mechanism for absorbing asymmetric shocks, no common unemployment insurance, and no fiscal capacity at the centre sufficient to act as a macroeconomic stabiliser. The result was a decade of austerity imposed on peripheral economies while surplus countries bore no comparable adjustment burden.

The Stability and Growth Pact Stability and Growth Pact A set of fiscal rules adopted in 1997 committing eurozone members to deficits below 3% of GDP and debt below 60% of GDP. Frequently suspended or renegotiated; widely criticised for imposing pro-cyclical constraints without a corresponding stabilisation mechanism. was suspended during COVID-19 precisely because member states could not coordinate fiscal responses fast enough through the existing architecture. The Next Generation EU fund — a departure from the no-debt principle — represented, for the first time, an EU-level bond issuance of significant size (€750 billion).

That it required a global pandemic and the threat of EU disintegration to reach even that modest step is itself the argument.

III. The Democratic Argument

The standard charge against the EU is that it suffers from a democratic deficit Democratic deficit The perceived gap between the powers exercised at EU level and the democratic accountability of EU decision-makers to citizens. Used by both federalists (who argue the solution is more democracy at EU level) and eurosceptics (who argue powers should return to national parliaments). — that it makes consequential decisions without adequate democratic legitimacy. The federalist response is that this is precisely correct, and that the solution is not to reduce EU competences but to build genuine democratic institutions at the level at which decisions are actually made.

The European Parliament, directly elected since 1979, has seen its powers substantially expanded through successive treaty revisions — from a consultative assembly to a genuine co-legislator with the Council under the ordinary legislative procedure introduced at Lisbon. Yet the Parliament still cannot initiate legislation (that power rests exclusively with the Commission), cannot elect or dismiss the executive as a parliament properly understood should, and has no power to raise revenue.

IV. The Security Argument

Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on 24 February 2022 ended a thirty-year European security settlement and made the structural inadequacy of European defence cooperation immediately legible. The EU has no standing army, no common defence budget, and no command structure. The response to the invasion was, correctly, routed through NATO and through individual member-state decisions on arms supply. But the EU's inability to act as a collective security actor — to make binding decisions on military support, to pool procurement, or to speak with a single voice in security negotiations — is not a feature. It is a liability.

In 2022, EU member states collectively spent approximately €214 billion on defence

— a sum comparable in scale to the United States' NATO contribution — but fragmented across twenty-seven separate procurement systems, twenty-seven separate command structures, and twenty-seven separate strategic doctrines. The subsidiarity principle Subsidiarity The principle (Article 5(3) TEU) that the EU should act only where the objectives of an action cannot be sufficiently achieved by member states alone. Often invoked against EU competence-expansion; federalists argue it equally justifies centralising security and fiscal decisions that member states demonstrably cannot achieve alone. argument actually cuts in favour of collective defence: no individual EU member is large enough to provide its own strategic deterrence.

V. Objections & Responses

What follows is a summary of the strongest objections to European federalism, and the responses they warrant. This section is here because a serious argument is made stronger, not weaker, by engaging with its critics honestly.

"A federation would mean the end of national sovereignty."

All federation involves the pooling, not the elimination, of sovereignty. Member states of the United States, Germany, Canada, and Switzerland retain substantial autonomous powers — in culture, education, local taxation, and much else — while sharing a federal authority in foreign policy, defence, and macroeconomics. The question is not whether sovereignty is shared (it already is, substantially, through the EU's existing acquis) but whether the sharing is adequately governed and democratically accountable. As things stand, it is neither.

"European citizens don't want this — there's no demos."

The "no demos" argument — that a European federation requires a shared European political identity that does not yet exist — confuses cause and effect. Political identity is partly a product of shared institutions: American national identity was not a precondition of the 1787 Constitution but in part a consequence of it. More concretely, Eurobarometer surveys have shown majority support for deeper EU integration in most member states in every wave since the late 1990s.

The question is whether political leadership is willing to make the argument.

"The EU already has a democratic deficit — federation would make it worse."

This objection runs the argument backwards. The EU's democratic deficit is a consequence of its current hybrid structure — in which executive power rests with a Council of national governments operating largely behind closed doors, while the directly elected Parliament lacks legislative initiative and revenue-raising powers. A federal settlement would, by design, transfer executive accountability to a legislature that is genuinely representative and transparent. The deficit is not inherent to supranationality; it is inherent to the specific, unfinished form the EU currently takes.

"Subsidiarity means decisions should stay national."

The subsidiarity principle (Article 5(3) TEU) requires that the EU act only where "the objectives of the proposed action cannot be sufficiently achieved by the Member States." For defence, macroeconomic stability, climate, and migration, this test is clearly met: these are cross-border challenges that individual member states demonstrably cannot address alone. Subsidiarity does not mandate national action; it mandates action at the level most capable of achieving the objective. In many of the domains where the EU acts most inadequately, subsidiarity is an argument for, not against, federal competence.
